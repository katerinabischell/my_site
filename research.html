📄 Manuscript in Preparation · 2026 With Dr. Arturo Keller, UCSB

Heavy Metal Contamination in the Pilcomayo River Basin

Spatiotemporal Dynamics, Sediment-Water Partitioning, and Geochemically Targeted Remediation Strategies

Bischel, K., and Keller, A. (in preparation). Heavy metal contamination in the Pilcomayo River Basin: Spatiotemporal dynamics, sediment-water partitioning, and geochemically targeted remediation strategies. [Journal TBD].

Collaborator: Dr. Arturo Keller, Bren School of Environmental Science & Management, UC Santa Barbara

This manuscript investigates heavy metal contamination dynamics in the Pilcomayo River Basin, examining how metals partition between sediment and water across space and time. The research develops geochemically targeted remediation strategies grounded in field data and environmental chemistry — directly advancing practical approaches to river pollution remediation.

The Pilcomayo River flows through Bolivia, Argentina, and Paraguay, serving as a critical water source for indigenous communities and agricultural regions while facing severe contamination from upstream mining activity. Quantifying spatiotemporal dynamics and sediment-water partitioning behavior is foundational to designing effective, site-specific remediation interventions.

Field Research · Machine Learning · 2025 📰 Featured In Press

Automated Pollinator Monitoring Using YOLOv8 Computer Vision

UC Santa Barbara · Cheadle Center for Biodiversity & Ecological Restoration · Dr. Katja Seltmann & Dr. Chris Evelyn

Developed and trained YOLOv8 object detection models to automate pollinator monitoring for two federally endangered coastal plant species: Ventura marsh milk-vetch (Astragalus pycnostachyus var. lanosissimus) and salt marsh bird's-beak (Chloropyron maritimum ssp. maritimum) at the North Campus Open Space (NCOS) restoration site in Goleta, CA.

Collected and processed 200+ hours of field camera footage over a 9-week season (June–September 2025) across four monitoring sites. Using Panasonic Lumix cameras positioned within yarn-delineated quadrants, footage was captured during three daily shifts three days per week, with site visit order randomized each shift. Environmental conditions were logged alongside each recording session.

Integrated trained models with the HyDaT tracking framework to identify and quantify pollinator visitation events, including entry timing, duration, and exit. Mentored two undergraduate research trainees throughout the season in field protocols, camera operation, and data quality assessment.
